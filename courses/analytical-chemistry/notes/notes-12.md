### Comparison: Precipitation Titration vs. Gravimetric Analysis

**Precipitation Titration:**
- Based on reaction forming precipitate
- Find unknown [Cl⁻] from $V_e$ of titrant [Ag⁺]
- $[\text{Cl}^-] = ?\ M$, $V_0 = 25\ \text{mL}$, $[\text{Ag}^+] = 0.05\ M$

**Question:** Are there other methods?

---

### Gravimetric Analysis Definition

**Definition:** Quantitative analysis based on weighing a precipitate.

**Example: Analysis of Chloride**

$$\text{Cl}^- + \text{Ag}^+ \rightarrow \text{AgCl}↓$$

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Unknown quantity** | Cl⁻ |
| **Precipitant** | Ag⁺ (in excess) |
| **Precipitate** | AgCl (weighing) |

**Gravimetric calculation** relates moles of product to moles of analyte.

## 2.6-1 Precipitation Process

### Experimental Stages in Precipitation

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **(a) Induction stage** | Time from mixing reagents to nucleation starting |
| **(b) Nucleation stage** | Formation of nuclei |
| **(c) Crystallite growth** | Crystal growth |

---

### What Determines Final Crystal Size?

**Crystal size vs. initial concentration of solution:**

| Solution Concentration | Crystal Size |
|------------------------|--------------|
| Concentrated solutions | Small crystals |
| Diluted solutions | Large crystals |

---

### Von Weimarn's Discovery

**As the relative supersaturation is lower, the crystals are larger.**

Von Weimarn found that the final crystal size is **inversely proportional** to the relative supersaturation of the solution.

**Relative Supersaturation:**
$$\text{Relative Supersaturation} = \frac{Q - S}{S}$$

Where:
- $Q$: Initial reagent concentration (just after mixing, before precipitation)
- $S$: Final solution concentration (at equilibrium conditions)

---

### Optimal Precipitation for Gravimetric Analysis

**Strategy:**
1. Slowly increase concentration to instability border
2. A few nucleons are formed
3. Add reagents during crystallization, keeping concentration within metastable band
4. No new nucleons are formed
5. Final crystals are large

---

### Temperature and Concentration Effects

| Condition | Supersaturation | Nucleons | Crystallites |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|--------------|
| Low $Q - S$ | Low | Few | Large |
| High $Q - S$ | High | Many | Small |
| Optimal | Controlled | Controlled | Large |

**Solution states:**
- **Unsaturated solution:** No precipitation
- **Metastable solution:** Controlled precipitation
- **Unstable solution:** Rapid precipitation

---

### Problem: High Local and Temporal Concentration

**During reagent mixing:**
- High $(Q - S)$ → many nucleons

**Example:**
$$\text{K}_2\text{CrO}_4 + \text{Ba(NO}_3)_2 \rightarrow \text{BaCrO}_4↓$$

---

### Solution: Homogeneous Precipitation

**Example:**
$$\text{HgCl}_2 + 2\text{NaI} \rightarrow \text{HgI}_2(s) + 2\text{NaCl}$$

**$(Q - S)$ is kept low to obtain large crystals.**

## 2.6-2 Homogeneous Precipitation

**Key principles:**
1. If precipitation is rapid, nucleation tends to dominate → many small particles
2. Make precipitation slow → particle growth dominates over nucleation → larger particles

**Definition:** Precipitant is produced slowly from an initially homogeneous solution by chemical reaction.

---

### Regular vs. Homogeneous Precipitation

**Example: Precipitation of Fe(OH)₃**

| Method | Characteristics |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Regular** | Rapid, many small particles |
| **Homogeneous** | Slow, larger particles |

## 2.6-3 Crystalline or Colloidal Precipitates?

| Type | Size |
|------|------|
| **Crystalline precipitate** | > 0.1 mm |
| **Colloidal precipitate** | 1 - 1000 nm |

**Colloids** are larger than most molecules but too small to crystallize.

---

### Colloidal Precipitation: Tyndall Effect

**How to determine a colloidal solution?**

**Tyndall effect** is an easy way of determining if a mixture is colloidal or not, based on light scattering.

**Colloids** are suspended by Brownian motion (random movement) of solvent molecules.

---

### Examples of Tyndall Effect in Nature

- **Crepuscular (Jesus) rays**
- **God's Eye / Jacob's Ladder**

The colloidal dispersion is dust or droplet (aerosol).

---

### Problems with Colloids

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Cannot be filtered (almost) | Understanding colloidal structure |
| Cannot be weighed (almost) | Optimal precipitation procedure |

**Very low-solubility materials** (low $S$) such as Fe(OH)₃ precipitate at high relative supersaturation and form colloids.

---

### Electric Double Layers for Colloid

**Structure:**

| Layer | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1st ionic layer** | Crystal ion which is in excess; Strongly bonded to crystal |
| **2nd ionic layer** | Charge opposite to 1st layer; Weak electrostatic bonds to 1st layer |

**Example: AgCl with excess NaCl**
- 1st layer: Cl⁻ (negatively charged particle)
- 2nd layer: Na⁺ (positively charged ionic atmosphere)

---

### Colloidal Particle Repulsion

**Colloidal particles repel one another** due to electrostatic repulsion.

**Particles must have enough kinetic energy to overcome electrostatic repulsion to coalesce.**

**Solutions:**
1. Ending precipitation with no reagent excess
2. Heating promotes coalescence through collision
3. Increasing electrolyte concentration

**Dielectric constant ($\xi$)** increases with electrolyte concentration, thus electrostatic forces $F$ become less:
$$F = \kappa\frac{d_1 d_2}{r^2}; \quad \kappa = \frac{1}{4\pi\xi}$$

---

### Examples of 2nd Ionic Layer

| Condition | Structure | Repulsion |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Excess Cl⁻** | ClAg:Cl⁻ ··· Na⁺ | Strong |
| **Excess Ag⁺** | AgCl:Ag⁺ ··· NO₃⁻ | Weak |

## 2.6-4 Washing and Filtering

### Questions:
1. **Why do we wash the precipitate?**
   - Remove droplets of liquid containing excess solute

2. **What happens upon washing with pure water?**
   - Peptization and losses upon filtration

3. **Which liquid can be used for washing?**
   - Water or electrolyte? Why?

---

### Peptization During Washing

**Before washing:**
- Coagulated precipitate
- 2nd ionic layer is tight

**During washing with pure water:**
- 2nd ionic layer is diluted
- Charged solid particles repel one another
- Precipitate breaks up → **peptization**

---

### Selecting the Washing Liquid

**Requirements:**
1. Electrolyte with an ion identical to the 2nd ionic layer (e.g., NO₃⁻)
2. Use volatile electrolyte, so it will evaporate during drying

**Examples:** HNO₃, NH₄NO₃ as washing liquid for AgCl

**Reactions:**
$$\text{AgCl:H}^+ \cdots \text{NO}_3^- \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{AgCl}↓ + \text{HNO}_3↑$$
$$\text{AgCl:NH}_4^+ \cdots \text{NO}_3^- \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} \text{AgCl}↓ + \text{NH}_3↑ + \text{HNO}_3↑$$

---

### Washing Strategy: One Large Portion vs. Many Small Portions

**Question:** Which is preferred?
- A: 1 Liter (one large portion)
- B: 10 × 0.1 Liter (many small portions)

---

### Contaminants After Washing Cycles

**Formula:**
$$X_n = X_0 \left(\frac{u}{u + v}\right)^n$$

Where:
- $X_n$: Contaminants after $n$ washing cycles
- $X_0$: Initial contaminants
- $u$: Volume of precipitate's liquid
- $v$: Volume used for each washing cycle
- $n$: Number of washing cycles

---

### Comparison: One Large vs. Many Small Washings

**One washing with volume $V$:**
$$X_1 = X_0 \left(\frac{u}{u + V}\right)$$

**$n$ washings with volume $v = V/n$:**
$$X_n = X_0 \left(\frac{u}{u + V/n}\right)^n$$

**For $n = 1$:** Expressions are identical

**For $n > 1$:** Always $X_n < X_1$

**Conclusion:** Many small portions are more effective than one large portion!

---

### Heating the Precipitate

**Purposes:**
1. Evaporation of water
2. Evaporation of electrolyte in washing
3. Converting to a component for weighing

---

### Choosing the Heating Temperature

**Example: Thermogram for CaC₂O₄·H₂O**

**Formula Weight:** 146.11 g/mol

**Heating stages (room temperature to 1000°C):**

| Temperature Range | Loss | Residue |
|-------------------|------|---------|
| First stage | Loss of H₂O(g) | CaC₂O₄ |
| Second stage | Loss of CO(g) | CaCO₃ |
| Third stage | Loss of CO₂(g) | CaO |

## Summary

- **Gravimetric analysis:** Quantitative analysis by weighing precipitate
- **Precipitation process:** Induction → Nucleation → Crystal growth
- **Crystal size:** Inversely proportional to relative supersaturation $(Q-S)/S$
- **Homogeneous precipitation:** Slow precipitant formation for larger crystals
- **Colloids:** 1-1000 nm, detected by Tyndall effect
- **Electric double layer:** Causes colloidal repulsion
- **Washing:** Many small portions better than one large; use volatile electrolyte
- **Heating:** Convert to stable weighing form
